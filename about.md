John Keats (1795-1821) was an English poet of the "second generation" of Romantic poets. His first poem, "Imitation of Spenser," was written in 1814 when he was 19 years old. This was the beginning of his short career as a published poet before his death of tuberculosis at the age of 25.

Keats published three volumes of poetry in his lifetime: <i>Poems</i> (1817), <i>Endymion</i> (1818), and <i>Lamia, Isabella, The Eve of St. Agnes, and Other Poems</i> (1820). The latter volume contains Keats's celebrated odes of 1819, including "Ode on a Grecian Urn," "Ode to a Nightingale," and, the subject of this website, "To Autumn."

"To Autumn" was composed between September 19 and 21, 1819 and is today considered one of the great poems in English literature. The poem as it is read today has undergone a series of transformations and corrections since it was first put to paper. This digital edition contains the standard version of the poem alongside two earlier versions written in Keats's hand. It is the goal of this edition to enable casual and scholarly readers of the poem to see the creative process at work between these versions of the poem, demonstrating how Keats crafted what was to become one of the most quoted poems in the English language. These early versions show the attention to detail paid by Keats to his word choice and syntax, while also showing his apparent disregard for conventional spelling and punctuation. These eccentricities have been supplemented with footnotes from past editors of the poem. These three versions and their annotations provide a more complete version of this beloved poem, opening up new avenues for appreciation and study.
